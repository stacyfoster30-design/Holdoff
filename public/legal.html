❓What Is HoldOff, Really?

HoldOff is a relationship awareness app designed to help you pause and reflect before sending messages when emotions run high. It uses pattern recognition to flag language commonly seen in mental health struggles (like rejection sensitivity, anxiety spirals, or attachment issues), but it does this with a clear understanding: pattern recognition is not diagnosis.

HoldOff was created by Stacy Ann Martin, a person with lived experience of ADHD, rejection sensitive dysphoria, and relationship communication challenges. She built HoldOff to help herself and others think twice before sending messages that might escalate conflict.

The bottom line: HoldOff is a tool that helps you pause and choose better. It's not therapy, it's not a diagnosis, and it's not a cure. But it might help you send fewer messages you regret.

🔍Pattern Detection Disclaimer

What the System Does

HoldOff uses pattern recognition to flag language that commonly appears in:

  • Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD)
  • Anxiety-driven spirals
  • Avoidant attachment responses
  • Emotional dysregulation patterns
  • Perfectionism rumination
  • Other common mental health presentations

What It Does NOT Do

  • Does NOT diagnose — Flags are observations, not diagnoses
  • Does NOT predict outcomes — Pattern recognition ≠ certainty
  • Does NOT replace professional assessment — Only a licensed professional can diagnose
  • Does NOT guarantee accuracy — Some flags will be false positives or misses

How to Use Flags Responsibly

  1. Pause & reflect — Take time to consider why the message triggered
  2. Don't shame yourself — Mental health patterns are neurological, not character flaws
  3. Seek professional help if needed — If patterns are interfering with relationships, talk to a therapist
  4. Use as awareness, not diagnosis — "This looks like RSD" ≠ "I have RSD"
  5. Trust your judgment — You can override any flag and send anyway
Important: HoldOff's pattern detection is based on research from Cleveland Clinic, WebMD, ADDitude, and peer-reviewed studies. However, it has not been clinically validated across diverse populations and may be less accurate for different demographics, cultures, or communication styles.
