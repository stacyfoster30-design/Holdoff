How to Stop Checking Your Phone Waiting for a Text

You've checked it 11 times in the last 20 minutes. Here's how to stop.

Why You Can't Stop Checking

Intermittent reinforcement — the same mechanism that makes slot machines addictive — is operating here. Sometimes when you check, there's a message. Sometimes there isn't. The uncertainty is what keeps you checking. Your brain is running a reward loop.

What Makes It Worse

  • Checking triggers more anxiety, which triggers more checking
  • Each check that finds nothing re-activates the threat signal
  • The behavior signals to your brain that this is an emergency worth monitoring

Breaking the Loop

The most effective intervention is physical separation — put the phone in a different room. Not face-down beside you. A different room. The "out of sight" effect is real and significant. Your nervous system de-escalates when there's no object to check.

What to Do With the Next 30 Minutes

Something with your hands. Cooking, walking, cleaning, drawing. Not reading (you'll just think about it) and not scrolling (you'll check the app). Physical activity is the fastest way to move the anxious energy out of your body.

Common Questions

Is it normal to check my phone this often waiting for a text?

Common — especially if the relationship is new or in an uncertain phase. Anxious attachment styles are particularly prone to this. Normal doesn't mean helpful, though.

Does turning off read receipts help?

It removes one anxiety trigger — but it can also lead to different forms of checking (last active time, typing indicators). Treat the source, not just the symptom.

Should I tell them I'm waiting?

In some relationships, yes — that's a reasonable and direct thing to say. 'I notice I'm checking my phone a lot today, which is a sign I'd love to hear from you.' But that only works if the relationship can handle directness.
